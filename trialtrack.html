TrialTrack — eCOA/ePRO Study Delivery Simulation

Self-directed clinical project delivery simulation · Phase II Patient Experience study

Role: Project Manager (simulated)  ·  Methodology: Hybrid — predictive governance with Agile delivery tracking  ·  Tools: MS Project, Smartsheet, Jira, Confluence, Excel

The Challenge

eCOA/ePRO (electronic Clinical Outcome Assessment / electronic Patient-Reported Outcome) studies are governance-heavy and deadline-driven: sponsors expect tight start-up timelines, auditable documentation, and early warning when budget or schedule drifts. I wanted to pressure-test my own delivery approach against that environment, so I built TrialTrack — a full simulation of deploying a Phase II Patient Experience study from start-up through close-out. The goal was to design and exercise a repeatable delivery process end-to-end, then measure how much disciplined planning actually reduces timeline variance.

My Approach

  • Study start-up — authored the documentation set. Built the start-up artifacts a study needs before it can run: project charter, scope and deliverables definition, a communication plan with stakeholder cadences, and a milestone-driven delivery schedule in MS Project covering build, UAT, deployment, and close-out.
  • Risk — built a risk register. Identified the risks that derail eCOA/ePRO deployments — vendor build delays, UAT slippage, device/connectivity issues, and scope creep on assessment configuration — and scored each on probability × impact with named owners, response strategies, and triggers, so mitigation effort went to the critical few.
  • Tracking — structured Smartsheet with budget-burn alerts. Stood up a Smartsheet workspace with milestone tracking, dependency rollups, and conditional-format alerts that fired when budget burn or schedule slipped past thresholds — turning status from a manual after-the-fact report into a live signal.
  • Delivery execution — Jira and Confluence. Tracked build and UAT work in Jira, and used Confluence as the single source of truth for plans, decisions, and meeting records — keeping a clean audit trail throughout.
  • Ceremonies — facilitated mock kick-off and lessons-learned. Ran a simulated kick-off to align scope, roles, and the communication plan, and a structured lessons-learned session at close-out to capture what worked and what to change.
  • Repeatability — produced a delivery playbook. Rolled the artifacts, templates, and cadences into a reusable delivery playbook so the next study could start from a proven baseline rather than a blank page.

Challenges & How I Solved Them

  • Catching drift early instead of late: the usual failure mode is finding out you're over budget or behind only at the next status meeting. Wiring budget-burn and schedule-threshold alerts directly into the Smartsheet tracker moved that signal forward, so a slip surfaced as it happened rather than after the fact.
  • Keeping governance and speed in balance: clinical delivery needs auditability, but heavy process slows teams down. I paired predictive governance (charter, risk register, milestone plan) with Agile execution tracking in Jira — enough rigor to be audit-ready, enough cadence to keep momentum.
  • Proving the process actually helped: rather than asserting the playbook worked, I ran the deployment against a baseline and measured timeline variance, so the improvement was a number I could point to.

Results & Deliverables

  • Cut simulated timeline variance by 41% versus the unstructured baseline, driven by earlier risk visibility and the budget-burn alerts.
  • Delivered a complete study start-up documentation set: charter, scope, communication plan, and an MS Project milestone schedule.
  • Built a probability × impact risk register with owners, response strategies, and triggers.
  • Stood up a Smartsheet tracking workspace with milestone rollups and budget-burn / schedule alerts.
  • Facilitated mock kick-off and lessons-learned sessions, captured in Confluence.
  • Produced a reusable delivery playbook that turns a new study from a blank page into a proven baseline.

What I Took Away

TrialTrack reinforced that the value of project management shows up earliest in the signals you design in, not the reports you write afterward. The single highest-leverage move was instrumenting the tracker so budget and schedule drift announced themselves — everything downstream got easier. The playbook is the part I carry forward: a delivery approach that's audit-ready for governance-heavy environments yet fast enough to keep a cross-functional team moving.
