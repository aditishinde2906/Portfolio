NavBharat — Risk Management & Project Plan for a 300 MW Coal Power Plant

Graduate project management case study · Northeastern University · Group 1

Role: Project Manager  ·  Team: 4-person team  ·  Methodology: Traditional / Predictive (PMBOK-aligned)  ·  Tools: Excel, EVM

The Challenge

Nava Bharat Energy India Limited proposed two coal-fired power projects in Andhra Pradesh totaling 300 MW to help close India's electricity deficit — a ₹13.86 billion capital project financed with a 2.33:1 debt-equity structure (₹9.70B term loan + ₹4.16B equity). A project of this scale carries serious, interlocking risks: regulatory approval, financial closure, fuel supply, technology, and off-take. Our task was to act as the project's management team and produce a complete plan to scope, schedule, cost, and de-risk the build — and recommend whether it was a sound investment.

My Approach

  • Scope — built the Work Breakdown Structure. Decomposed the entire project into five phases (Project Management, Initiation, Planning, Execution, Closure) with detailed work packages and milestones — from governance and the project charter, through site selection, financing, procurement, construction at both Paloncha and Dharmavaram sites, power generation and distribution, down to lessons-learned and formal closure.
  • Cost & schedule — produced ROM estimates. Attached rough-order-of-magnitude cost and duration estimates to every work package (e.g. machinery procurement at $8–10B, construction at $6–8B), rolling up to the overall budget and a ~30-month timeline (planned start June 30, 2009; modeled completion ~December 2011).
  • Schedule risk — ran a quantitative confidence analysis. Used a probabilistic model to answer "what date can we be 80% confident of finishing?", surfacing regulatory approvals, technology development, and contract negotiation as the riskiest, most delay-prone paths.
  • Risk management — built the full framework. Identified risks via brainstorming, interviews, and documentation; analyzed each qualitatively and quantitatively; and prioritized them on a probability × impact threshold. Built a risk register with response strategies (mitigate / transfer / accept / avoid) and assigned owners — e.g. for fuel-supply disruption (scored High), the response was to diversify fuel sources, maintain coal stockpiles, and secure alternative transport (70% freight rail / 30% barge), owned by the COO.
  • Contingency — recommended a reserve. Calculated a 10% contingency reserve (~₹1.386 billion) against total project cost to absorb identified uncertainty.
  • Monitoring & control — planned EVM and ongoing governance. Defined tracking using Earned Value Management (Planned Value, Earned Value, Actual Cost), a living risk register, scheduled risk reviews, and stakeholder reporting cadences.

Challenges & How We Solved Them

  • Interlocking, high-stakes risks: with regulatory, financial, fuel, and technology risks all able to derail financial closure, we used a probability × impact threshold to separate the critical few from the many — so mitigation effort and the contingency reserve went where they mattered most.
  • Estimating under deep uncertainty: for a multi-year, multi-billion build, point estimates would have been misleading. We used ROM ranges plus a probabilistic schedule model to express confidence honestly (an 80%-confidence date) rather than a single optimistic deadline.
  • Making a defensible investment call: by quantifying risk and reserves, we could conclude that — despite the risk profile — the project remained a viable long-term investment given India's energy-demand growth, and back that recommendation with analysis rather than assertion.

Results & Deliverables

  • Delivered a complete project & risk management plan for a ₹13.86B infrastructure project: WBS, ROM cost/schedule estimates, probabilistic schedule analysis, prioritized risk register with response strategies and owners, a contingency-reserve recommendation, and an EVM-based monitoring plan.
  • Produced a multi-level WBS spanning five phases and 30+ work packages with cost and duration estimates.
  • Recommended a ₹1.386B (10%) contingency reserve grounded in the identified risk exposure.
  • Concluded with a risk-based investment recommendation, supported by quantitative analysis.

What I Took Away

This project was the predictive, heavy-infrastructure counterpart to my Agile product work — and it sharpened the disciplines that travel everywhere: decompose scope before estimating, express schedule as confidence rather than a single date, prioritize risk by probability × impact, and fund a contingency reserve deliberately. I apply the same rigor to software delivery, just on a faster cadence.
