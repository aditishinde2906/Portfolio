FundFlow — Personal Finance Management App

Graduate product project · Northeastern University (PJM6825: Agile Lean Product Development)

Role: Project Manager  ·  Team: 4-person cross-functional team (Legal Eliminators)  ·  Timeline: ~2.5 months  ·  Methodology: Agile / Lean

The Challenge

Many young professionals (ages 18–35) struggle to manage personal finances — budgeting, tracking expenses, paying down debt, and saving toward goals. Existing apps are often too complex or time-consuming for daily use, and lack personalized goal-setting and progress tracking. Our team was tasked with taking a finance-app idea from problem to a validated MVP concept in a single semester, applying Agile and Lean product-development practices the way a real product team would.

My Approach

  • Framed the problem and target market — defined the core user pain (low financial literacy, fragmented accounts, overspending) and segmented to a primary persona: young professionals juggling student loans, rent, and early investing.
  • Ran a market-needs analysis — mapped each user need against its under-served gap (budgeting that's too complex, goal-tracking that can't handle multiple goals, debt tools without actionable insight), grounding every feature decision in a real gap.
  • Prioritized the MVP with a structured framework — sorted features into Basic (secure sign-in, goal setting, transaction management, data import), Performance (monthly budgets, bank-account connection, automated categorization, expense notifications), and Delighters (insights & visual reports, goal-progress tracking, university-system integration).
  • Defined the feature set and user flows — translated needs into six key functionalities and coordinated wireframes for the core journeys: onboarding/account setup, a "Know Your Customer" goal-setting flow, expense tracking, budget dashboard, and a security/settings page.
  • Established a measurement framework — defined both success metrics (DAU/MAU, session duration, savings increase, debt reduction, budget adherence, NPS, CSAT, module completion) and failure metrics, so the product's health could be judged objectively post-launch.

Challenges & How We Solved Them

  • Scope creep risk: the feature wishlist was large. I used a structured prioritization framework to force trade-offs, cutting "nice-to-haves" out of the MVP so the core experience stayed simple — directly answering the under-served need that competing apps are too complex.
  • Keeping decisions evidence-based: rather than designing features we liked, we tied every functionality back to a documented user need and its market gap, so the MVP scope was defensible.
  • Coordinating a 4-person cross-functional team: with teammates owning problem research, personas, wireframes, and metrics, I sequenced the workstreams over the 2.5-month timeline, ran check-ins to keep everyone unblocked, and aligned the parallel pieces into one coherent product narrative and final deliverable.

Results & Deliverables

  • Delivered a complete 0→1 product concept package: problem definition, target persona, market-needs analysis, prioritized MVP, full wireframe set, and a success/failure metrics framework.
  • Produced clickable-quality wireframes for 5 core flows (onboarding, goal-setting, expense tracking, budget dashboard, settings/security).
  • Defined a measurable success framework spanning engagement, financial-outcome, satisfaction, and financial-literacy metrics.

What I Took Away

This project mirrored a real product lifecycle: validating a problem, scoping ruthlessly, designing flows around documented needs, and committing to metrics before building. It's how I now approach any feature — start from the under-served need, protect a simple MVP, and define success up front.
